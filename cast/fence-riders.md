> *"A staple tells you who drove it. A cut wire tells you who paid for it. The dirt underneath tells you who lied about both."*

## Wire and Boundary Men

The fence rider works alone, mostly. He follows a line of posts and wire across country that was open range a decade ago, patrolling for breaks, stock drift, trampled gates, and the kind of trouble that begins with pliers and ends with a rifle. He carries bent staples in his coat pocket, a hammer on his saddle, and opinions about every property boundary between French Gulch and the Shasta road that nobody in the courthouse wants to hear.

Fence riders serve ranch owners, mining companies, and sometimes both in the same week. They are not law, but they enforce a kind of order written in barbed wire and hoof churn. A good fence rider can tell you who cut a line by the tool marks and who drove the cattle through by the shoe pattern. A bad one just patches the wire and hopes the shooting starts after his shift.

### Role

Range patrol, boundary enforcement, stock accountability, and water-access judgment along disputed fence lines.

### Traits

- **Wire-scarred hands.** No fence rider works a season without barbed-wire cuts on his palms and forearms. The scars are a record of employment.
- **Rides the line at first light.** Stock drifts overnight, wire gets cut in the dark, and gates get left open by men who swear they closed them. Morning is when the evidence is fresh.
- **Loyal to the post, not the owner.** A fence rider will serve a man he despises if the pay is honest, and quit a man he respects if the pay stops.
- **Knows every water hole by name.** Whoever controls the water controls the stock, and whoever controls the stock controls the argument.

### Trail Work

#### Cut-Wire Report

When a fence rider finds a break, he must decide what to report and to whom. Was it weather, an animal, or a man with pliers? The answer decides whether the owner sends a repair crew or a posse.

#### Stock Drift

Cattle wander through broken fence, and the fence rider must account for every head — whose brand, which direction, how long loose. Returning strays to the right owner is diplomacy. Returning them to the wrong owner is theft.

#### Boundary Testimony

When two ranchers dispute a line, the fence rider who patrols it may be the only witness to where the posts actually stand. His word in front of a county judge can settle or ignite a range war.

#### Water-Hole Watch

A fenced water hole is a weapon. The fence rider who controls access decides which stock drinks and which stock dies. He can open a gate for a neighbor's herd or keep it shut and call it policy.

#### Night Patrol

Wire gets cut after dark. The fence rider who rides the line by lantern light sees things the daytime patrol misses — fresh boot prints, horse droppings still warm, the smell of tobacco where no one should be smoking.

#### Fence Mend

Carrying wire, staples, and a stretcher bar, the fence rider patches what he finds broken. A clean mend is invisible. A sloppy mend tells every passing rider that the line is weak and the owner is careless.

#### Hidden Tracks

A fence rider reads the ground like a ledger. Hoof prints, boot prints, drag marks, and wagon ruts near a fence line tell a story that the people involved would prefer stayed buried.

#### Gate-Chain Trouble

A locked gate is only as honest as the man who holds the key. Fence riders know which gates get opened by arrangement, which get opened by bribery, and which get opened by a hacksaw at midnight.

#### Widow's Wire

When a rancher dies and his widow cannot afford a fence rider, the line goes slack. Stock drifts, neighbors encroach, and the boundary becomes a suggestion. A fence rider who helps for free earns respect. A fence rider who reports the weakness earns pay from the wrong quarter.

#### Grudge Post

Some fence lines exist not to hold stock but to make a point — a post driven through a disputed spring, a wire strung across a road somebody else claims. The fence rider who maintains a grudge fence is doing political work, whether he admits it or not.

### Camp Say

> *"I have mended wire for men who steal and cut wire for men who pray. The fence does not care who is righteous. It only cares whether the staple holds."*
>
> — A fence rider knows that every boundary is an argument the land has not yet settled.
