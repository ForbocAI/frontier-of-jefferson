## Old Pack Talk

Wolves were never common in Jefferson country, and by 1899 most folks will tell you they are gone entirely. But gone is not the same as absent. Every hard winter brings a fresh set of claims: a howl heard from the rimrock after dark, a dead yearling with its throat opened in a manner no coyote could manage, tracks in the snow too wide for any ranch dog. The trouble is that half these reports come from men who want the bounty, a quarter come from men who want an excuse for lost stock, and the rest come from old hunters whose eyes are not what they were. What remains, once the lies are sorted out, is a thin and stubborn truth — something still moves through the high timber, and it does not move like a dog.

The rumor of wolves does its own work long before any animal is confirmed. Stock prices shift when a cattleman tells the wrong story at the wrong auction. Patrol routes change when a foreman claims sign on a neighbor's boundary. Permission to cross private land dries up the moment someone names the word *wolf* within earshot of a man who has calves on open ground. The rumor travels farther than any pack and bites nearly as hard.

### Role

**Rare sign and disputed predator** — a source of uncertainty that changes what people do before they know what is true.

### Traits

- **Tracks that don't match** — prints too large for coyote, too straight-lined for dog, found where no one expected them and argued over before the mud dries.
- **The distant howl** — heard once, at dusk or before dawn, from a ridge no one wants to climb in the dark. Never heard twice from the same place.
- **Missing stock with no witness** — a calf gone from a pen, a ewe dragged into brush, and nothing left but drag marks and a story that grows with every telling.
- **Old hunter certainty** — a man who saw wolves forty years ago and swears he knows the difference, even when no one else believes him.

### Rumors and Signs

#### A Print in the Frost

A single clear track appears in frozen mud near a stock pen. It is large, deep-clawed, and perfectly spaced. Anyone who sees it will have an opinion. The man who owns the pen wants it called a dog. The man who wants the bounty wants it called a wolf. The track itself does not care. Whoever names it first sets the terms for the next week of conversation in town.

#### The Hayden Calf

A yearling calf is found dead below the tree line on Hayden's lease, throat torn and hindquarters partly eaten. Coyotes have been at the remains since, which makes reading the kill difficult. Hayden blames wolves. His neighbor says it was dogs. The hide buyer in town says it was a cougar. Each answer costs someone different money, and none of them were there when it happened.

#### Howl Off the Rimrock

A freight driver reports hearing a long howl from the rimrock east of Dillon Creek, just before full dark. He is a steady man and not given to invention, but he was also alone and tired. If his story holds, it means something is denning in country that three different outfits use for summer pasture. If his story does not hold, he has still made those outfits nervous for no gain.

#### The Bounty Question

County talk turns to whether the old wolf bounty — two dollars a head, last paid out in 1891 — ought to be renewed. Those in favor say it worked before and will work again. Those against say there are no wolves left to bounty and the money would only draw liars with dog pelts and coyote ears. The question itself reveals who believes and who does not, which is worth more than the answer.

#### Snow Reading

After a late storm, a rider finds a line of tracks crossing open meadow in a straight, purposeful gait — not the wandering pattern of a dog or the darting line of a coyote. The tracks enter timber on the far side and do not come back out. He can follow them or he can leave them alone. Following means losing a day. Leaving means not knowing. Either choice has a price at the supper table.

#### Mistaken Dog Sign

A rancher shoots a large brindle dog he finds near his sheep pen at dawn, convinced it is a wolf. By midday he has told four people. By evening, the dog's owner — a man from two valleys over — arrives looking for his animal. The rancher now has a debt, a lie to maintain, and a neighbor who will not forget. The wolf, if there ever was one, is somewhere else entirely.

#### The Poison Argument

An older stockman proposes setting strychnine baits along the ridge where sign has been reported. Others object — not from sympathy for the animal, but because poison does not read brands. Baits kill ranch dogs, scavenger birds, and anything else that finds them first. The argument splits a camp meeting and leaves two men who used to share fence labor on opposite sides of a question neither can answer.

#### Sold Short at Auction

A cattleman brings thirty head to the Yreka auction and finds the price dropped since last month. The buyer mentions wolf talk and shrugs. It does not matter whether wolves killed anything — the rumor reached the auction house before the cattle did, and the buyer uses it to negotiate. The cattleman leaves with less money and more anger than he arrived with, and he will remember who started the story.

#### Permission Withdrawn

A trapper who has crossed the Garrett ranch freely for six years is told he can no longer use the east gate. Garrett says it is because of the wolf sign reported near his upper pasture — he does not want foot traffic pushing predators toward his stock. The trapper suspects the real reason is a disagreement over a shared water right. The wolf is a convenient door to close, and once closed, it will not open easily.

#### The Old Hunter's Testimony

Asa Kline, who is seventy-three and nearly deaf, tells anyone who will listen that he saw a grey wolf crossing the river bar below his cabin in October. He describes the size, the color, the gait, and the way it turned its head to look at him before it entered the willows. His account is precise, calm, and impossible to verify. Half the county thinks he is telling the truth. The other half thinks he is remembering 1866. What matters is that Asa will not change his story, and the men who dismiss him will not walk that river bar after dark.

### Camp Say

> "Could be wolves. Could be dogs. Could be three men lying for three different reasons and a dead calf that ain't talking. Point is, the calves are moved, the bounty talk has started, and nobody is sleeping quite right — and that was true before anyone proved a thing."
