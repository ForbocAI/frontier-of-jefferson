## Company Shotguns

![Company Shotguns](cast/images/company-shotguns.jpg)

> *"Any man drawing company wages and carrying company iron is advised that his conduct reflects upon the Harshaw Consolidated Mining & Timber concern. Damage to company property, including yourself, will be deducted from final pay. Discharge of firearms within the bunkhouse is grounds for dismissal without settlement of accounts."*
> — Posted notice, Guard Quarters, Harshaw Mine No. 3

## Mine Guard Men

They come from the places work has already chewed through. Failed ranches, closed mills, logging camps where the timber ran out and the paymaster left by night. Some wore a badge once and lost it. Some never did and never wanted one. What they share is a willingness to stand where told and look hard at whoever walks toward them, and for that the company pays better than the drift face and steadier than cattle.

A company shotgun is not the law. He knows it. The miners know it. The sheriff knows it and stays home when the trouble is on company ground. But a man with a ten-gauge and a letter from the superintendent can hold a road as long as nobody asks too careful whose authority he carries. The guards ride payroll wagons, walk the tipple at shift change, and stand thick at the gate when the union talk turns loud. Their loyalty lasts exactly as long as the pay envelope, and most of them understand this about themselves with a clarity that would be admirable if it weren't so dangerous.

### Role

Stand between the company's money and anyone the company would rather not pay.

### Traits

- Worn boots polished only at the toe-cap where the mud won't stick, coats too heavy for the season because the pockets carry weight
- A habit of counting men the way a drover counts cattle — by the gate, by the head, by the ones that drift
- Old injuries explained with short lies: a limp blamed on a horse, knuckles blamed on a door
- Eyes that check the hands before the face

### Trail Work

#### The Wage Line

A company shotgun can hold ground not because anyone respects him but because crossing him means crossing the payroll. When he stands at a gate or a bridge or a store porch, the question is never whether he has the right — it is whether the cost of moving him is worth what waits on the other side. He makes geography expensive.

#### Payroll Escort

Two men on a wagon with a lockbox and a scattergun apiece, twelve miles of bad road, and the knowledge that every soul in the camp knows what day the money moves. A payroll escort is a scene made of silence and arithmetic — who knows the route, who watches from the ridge, and whether the driver trusts the guard more than the guard trusts the driver.

#### Disarm and Detain

A company guard has no jail and no warrant, but he has a bunkhouse with a bolt on the outside and a foreman who will swear the man was drunk. Disarming a miner or a stranger on company land is half bluff and half geography: do it where no one sees, and it happened however the guard says it happened. Do it at the shift bell and there had better be enough guards to finish it.

#### Strike Pressure

When the miners put down their tools, the shotguns pick up theirs. A company guard during a strike is a man paid to stand in a road and be hated, and most of them discover feelings about that work they did not expect. The ones who stay hard get meaner. The ones who don't get quiet. Either way, the company wants the road open and the men back underground, and a guard who cannot deliver that is a guard looking for new employment in a town that already knows his face.

#### The False Arrest

There is no arrest — that would require a deputy. There is a suggestion, made with a hand on the shoulder and a shotgun held casual in the other arm, that a man might want to come along to the office and discuss matters with the superintendent. The suggestion works because refusing it is ugly and the walk is short. What happens at the office is between the man, the superintendent, and whatever the guard is willing to pretend he did not hear.

#### Fear Tax

A company shotgun does not need to hurt anyone if the town believes he will. Half the work is walking the same route at the same hour, standing in the same doorway, letting people build the story themselves. The fear is cheaper than the violence and lasts longer, but it spoils — eventually someone decides to test it, and then the guard must make good or lose the only tool that kept the peace.

#### The Tired Guard

Not every man with company iron in his hands believes in the work. Some took the job because the alternative was the drift face or the road, and they stand at the gate with the growing suspicion that the men on the other side of it are more like them than the men who sign the pay vouchers. A tired guard is a crack in the company wall — he might look away at the right moment, lose a key, forget a name, or simply walk off one morning and leave the gate standing open.

#### Conscience Under Pay

A guard who starts asking whether the work is right is a guard who has already half-quit. The question eats slow — it starts with a miner's wife carrying a sick child past the gate, or a boy too young for the drift face being told he owes the company store more than his father earned alive. The company does not pay for conscience and has no line on the voucher for doubt, but both show up anyway, uninvited, and sit heavy in a man who still needs the wage.

#### Choosing Not to Fire

The hardest moment for a company shotgun is the one where the crowd pushes close enough and angry enough that the ten-gauge would solve it, and he knows it, and they know it, and he does not pull. What keeps the finger off the trigger is rarely mercy — it is the knowledge that the sound will bring consequences the paycheck cannot cover. A guard who chooses not to fire has made a calculation, not a kindness, but the miners walking home alive do not care about the difference.

#### Desertion Wages

When a company guard quits, he does not give notice — he leaves the shotgun on the bunk, takes what the company store will sell him on the last day of credit, and walks. The company calls it desertion and docks the final pay. The guard calls it the first honest thing he has done since he pinned on the badge that is not a badge. The road out of a company town is the same road in, but it feels longer, and the man walking it is lighter by exactly the weight of a ten-gauge and whatever he told himself to carry it.

### Camp Say

> *"He ain't the law. He's what the law looks like when somebody buys it by the hour."*

That means: authority purchased is authority borrowed. The man holding it knows he will give it back, and so does everyone watching.
