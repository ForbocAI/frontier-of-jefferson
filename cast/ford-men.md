## Crossing Hands

Where Clear Creek bends wide enough for a wagon to attempt passage, the ford men stand hip-deep in judgment. They are the laborers who read current by ear, who know the bottom rock by season, and who decide — before your team sets hoof in the shallows — whether your load is honest, your axle sound, and your coin sufficient. They do not own the creek, but the creek answers to their memory, and no man crosses without their say unless he is willing to swim for it.

Some work a toll station backed by a road company. Others hold a ford by nothing more than local agreement and a reputation for pulling out the ones who tried alone. They haul rope, drive stakes, wade alongside panicked mules, and remember every face that ever lied about what was lashed under the tarp.

### Role

Crossing labor, toll judgment, and wagon-risk arbitration at creek fords along the road.

### Traits

- **Wet to the waist by noon.** Ford men carry the smell of creek mud, horse sweat, and soaked leather. Dry boots mean a man who has not worked yet today.
- **Counting before asking.** They size up a load by how the wagon sits, not by what the driver claims.
- **Short patience for bluster.** Talk does not move water. A ford man will walk away from a shouter and let the creek settle the argument.

### Trail Work

#### Toll Reckoning

A ford man names his price by the weight of the wagon, the height of the water, and the look on the driver's face. He can raise the toll if he smells trouble — or cut it for a widow hauling flour. Either choice makes enemies.

#### Current Read

Before any crossing, the ford man wades out alone to test depth, footing, and drift. If he comes back shaking his head, no amount of money will move him. If he waves the team forward, the cost of refusal is his reputation.

#### Hidden Cargo

Ford men learn to notice what does not match — a light wagon that rides heavy, a tarp lashed too tight for flour, a driver who will not let anyone near the bed. Knowing what rides underneath gives a ford man leverage he may not want.

#### Hoof Panic

When a horse loses footing in current, the ford man must grab the lead and hold the animal's head above water while the driver fights the reins. It is dangerous, thankless work. A man who does it well earns trust that money cannot buy.

#### Flood Watch

When the creek rises after rain, the ford man decides whether to close the crossing or let one last wagon through. That decision has killed ford men and saved towns. The water does not wait for committee.

#### Witness Memory

Every rider who crosses leaves a picture in a ford man's mind — face, horse, load, direction, and time of day. Sheriffs, bounty men, and worried wives all come asking. What a ford man remembers, and what he claims to forget, is its own kind of currency.

#### Rescue Toll

When a wagon overturns in the current, the ford man pulls out what he can — people first, goods second, animals if there is time. What he salvages, he may claim a share of. What he loses, he carries in his sleep.

#### Washed-Out Detour

A ruined ford sends traffic to the next crossing, sometimes miles upstream. The ford man who holds the only passable route after a storm can name any price, or he can let people through for nothing and collect the debt in winter.

#### Dawn Crosser

Some riders arrive before first light, hoping to cross without being seen or counted. A ford man who sleeps at his post loses toll money. A ford man who catches a dawn crosser learns what kind of business travels in the dark.

#### Rope and Stake

When the bottom shifts or the water cuts a new channel, the ford man must re-stake the crossing — driving posts into the creekbed, stringing guide ropes, marking the safe line with cairns or flags. It is slow, cold labor, and a poorly set stake will pull a horse under.

### Camp Say

> *"I do not care where you have been or where you aim to go. I care what your wagon weighs, what your horse thinks of the water, and whether you will look me in the eye when I ask what is under that canvas."*
>
> — A ford man measures you by what you are willing to show at the water's edge.
