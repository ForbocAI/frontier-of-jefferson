> "A dull axe costs you skin. A sharp one costs you judgment. Either way the brush don't care—it grew back thicker while you were arguing about the road."

## Choppers and Burn-Line Hands

They come with axes ground thin and boots already splitting at the sole. Brush crews open the county where it has grown shut—cutting fire line through manzanita so thick a dog couldn't push it, clearing road grade that hasn't seen a wagon since the last crew quit or died. The work is blisters on blisters, pitch in the hair, and smoke in the lungs by Tuesday. Some hands learned to read a burn from Karuk or Shasta neighbors who have tended these hills with fire longer than anyone drawing county maps. That knowledge is practical, earned across generations of watching what grows back and what stays clear, and the crews who listen to it cut less and finish sooner.

Others just swing until the brush is down and let the pile-burn sort the rest. Either way, what the crew finds under the tangle—a played-out camp, a boundary stake nobody filed, a bear trail punched six inches into the dirt—tends to matter more than the clearing itself. Half the feuds in the county started when a brush crew opened a line somebody wanted shut.

### Role

Hands who cut, burn, and open ground—and decide what gets reported when the brush comes down.

### Traits

- Pitch-black hands and a cough that won't quit past October
- Can tell green smoke from dry smoke at a quarter mile
- Counts days by blisters, not by calendar
- Sleeps in line camp and eats what the cook didn't burn worse than the brush

### Trail Work

#### Cut the Line Ahead of the Burn

Lay axe to the manzanita and scrub oak before fire gets put to the pile. You choose how wide, and every foot of width is another hour of labor. Cut it narrow and the fire jumps. Cut it wide and your hands are meat by sundown. The crew behind you is only as safe as your judgment of the wind.

#### Read the Smoke

Watch what color rises off the burn pile and how it bends. White smoke means wet wood and wasted time. Black means pitch and a fire that wants to run. Gray and low means the burn is doing its work. Misread it and the line you cut becomes the line you're running from. A hand who can read smoke keeps the crew working; one who can't keeps them digging escape.

#### Flush What Hides in the Tangle

Bear, cat, elk, and sometimes men—brush that thick holds what doesn't want to be found. When the axes start and the smoke rises, what's in there comes out. You can read the sign before you cut and know what's coming, or you can find out the hard way when a sow bear with cubs breaks through the crew at a dead run. Either way, you learn what the brush was keeping.

#### Find the Old Road

Under twenty years of new growth there's sometimes a grade—wagon ruts, culvert stones, even old blaze marks on the stumps. Finding it saves the county weeks of survey. But an old road found is an old road someone can use, and not everyone wants that. You choose whether to report the grade or let the brush grow back over it and say you found nothing.

#### Refuse the Burn

Sometimes the ground is wrong, the wind is wrong, or what's growing there shouldn't be put to fire at all. A hand who says "not today" costs the crew a day's wages and earns a foreman's grudge. But a hand who burns when the ground says don't can lose the whole south slope and the crew's reputation with it. The choice is yours, and the foreman will remember either way.

#### Work Someone Else's Claim Line

The brush doesn't know where one man's timber ends and another's starts, but the men do. Cut a foot past the boundary stake and you've given someone a cause of action—or done a neighbor a favor he didn't ask for. Either way, the surveyor's mark was under a log, and now it's in the open where lawyers can see it.

#### Tend a Burn the Old Way

Hands who've worked alongside Karuk or Shasta neighbors know there are places where low fire clears the brush and brings the basket grass back, and places where fire kills the soil for a decade. That's not magic—it's watching the same ground for longer than any homesteader has been here. Use what you've learned and the hillside comes back clean. Ignore it and you're cutting the same brush next year, if there's a next year.

#### Lose a Tool in the Slash

An axe-head flies off the haft. A mattock gets buried in a pile that's already burning. A crosscut saw bows and snaps on a hidden nail in an old stump. Every lost tool is a trip to town, a day without work, and a question about whether you can afford to keep on the crew. Some hands carry a spare. Most don't.

#### Uncover What Somebody Buried

Under the brush there are things: a cache of bottles, a rolled-up deed in an oilskin, a set of stakes that don't match the county record, a shallow pit with ash and bone that might be a cook-fire or might not be. What you do with it depends on who's paying for the clearing and whether you want to keep getting paid. The brush kept the secret. Now you have it.

#### Camp in the Line and Listen

After dark in line camp, the crew hears what the daytime cutting drowns out—the creek below, the coyotes working the ridgeline, the sound of somebody else's fire where nobody's supposed to be. A hand who listens learns where the water is, where the game moves, and who else is out in the hills pretending they aren't. Morning comes and you decide what to mention and what to keep to yourself.

### Camp Say

> "Brush'll grow back. The road won't build itself. And what you find under the tangle—well, that's between you and whoever lost it."

*The crew knows: the county is only as open as the last line they cut, and every clearing is somebody's secret turned to daylight.*
