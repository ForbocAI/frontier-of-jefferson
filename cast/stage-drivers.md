## Six-Horse Road Hands

They sit the box in all weather, twelve hours at a stretch, six lines of leather threaded through fingers that forgot how to close all the way years ago. Stage drivers and their shotgun guards are the veins of Jefferson State — carrying mail contracts, payroll strongboxes, passenger fares, and every rumor worth repeating between settlements that have no telegraph and no railroad spur. The company says keep to schedule. The road says otherwise. A driver learns to negotiate between the two or gets replaced by someone who will.

The guard rides with a sawed coach gun across his knees and one eye on the tree line. He is not there for the passengers. He is there for the green box under the seat. When the road turns, the driver and guard must decide together what matters more — the schedule, the cargo, or the lives behind the leather curtain. That decision gets made fast and lived with slow.

### Role

Move what the road will allow, on time or close to it, and bring the team home standing.

### Traits

- Hands mapped with rein burn, tobacco stain, and old fear worn white at the knuckle
- A voice pitched to carry over wheel noise, wind, and six horses pulling contrary
- The habit of reading mud, grade, and sky the way a clerk reads a ledger
- A silence after dark that means the driver is listening to what the horses hear

### Trail Work

#### Hold the Lines Through a Wash-Out

When the creek jumps the ford and the road disappears under brown water, the driver must read the current by how the leaders balk. Walk the team through or cut the traces and lose the schedule. Passengers will shout. The guard will watch the banks. The driver's hands decide.

#### Answer a Road-Agent Halt

Masked men in the timber call for the box. The guard can fight or comply. The driver keeps the team still — a panicked six-horse hitch in a narrow grade kills everyone, robbers included. The choice between the strongbox and the passengers is made in the time it takes to thumb back a hammer.

#### Run a Schedule Past Breaking

The company wants the mail in Jacksonville by Thursday. The road is soft, the off-wheeler is favoring her near fore, and the swing team hasn't watered since Grants Pass. Push the schedule and risk a breakdown in open country, or pull up and face the superintendent's letter. A driver's reputation is built on arrivals and destroyed by one lame horse.

#### Keep a Sick Passenger Alive to the Next Station

Fever, snakebite, or a bullet picked up two stops back — the driver cannot stop the coach for one fare, but cannot deliver a corpse without questions. The guard watches the sick one. The driver shaves time where the grade allows. The station keeper will have whiskey and maybe a doctor. Maybe.

#### Repair a Wheel on Grade

Iron tire throws on a rock shelf twelve miles from the nearest station. The driver and guard must lever the coach, reset the tire with what tools ride in the boot, and do it before dark brings worse company. Passengers can help or they can sit in the dust and complain. Either way, the wheel must turn.

#### Leverage the Mail Contract

The driver knows what is in the mail bags — not because he opens them, but because he knows who sends and who waits. That knowledge is worth favors in any settlement. A word about whose letter arrived, whose didn't, whose came back marked dead — that is currency no strongbox holds.

#### Read a False Passenger

Someone on the coach is not who the ticket says. The driver has carried enough fares to know when a man's hands don't match his clothes, when a woman watches the road behind instead of ahead. Whether to warn the guard, alert the next station, or keep silent depends on what kind of trouble the false passenger is likely to bring.

#### Abandon Cargo to Save the Team

A steep grade in winter, ice on the brake shoes, and the coach gaining on the wheelers. The driver can dump freight — mail bags, luggage, the strongbox itself — to lighten the load before the whole rig goes over the edge. The company will want an accounting. The passengers will want their belongings. The horses will want to live. The driver picks one.

#### Trade Rumors at the Relay Station

Ten minutes to change the team. The station keeper has heard things — who is hiring, who is dead, who rode through last night in a hurry. The driver trades his own road news for it. These exchanges move faster than any newspaper and carry more weight at the table. What the driver repeats and what he keeps shapes what the next settlement believes.

#### Bring a Dead Guard's Gun Home

The guard took a round at the Rogue River crossing. The driver finished the run alone, one hand on the lines and the other on the coach gun laid across his knees. Now he must deliver the weapon and the news to someone who will not want either. The company will send a replacement. The road will not wait.

### Camp Say

> "You can judge a road by what it does to a wheel, and a driver by what he does when the wheel gives. Schedule is a promise made to weather, horses, and every thief between here and the next station at the same time. Some days you keep all three. Most days you pick which one to break."
