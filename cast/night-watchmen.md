## Night Watchmen

![Night Watchmen](cast/images/night-watchmen.jpg)

> *"Three taps on the post means the alley is clear. No taps means somebody paid for quiet."*
> — chalk mark found inside the lid of a patrol lantern, Jefferson, 1899

## Lamp and Shotgun Men

They are not lawmen in any proper sense. Most carry no commission beyond a handshake and a weekly envelope. The night watchman walks his rounds because someone with property needs a man awake between midnight and first light — someone who will rattle a padlock, hold a lantern to a window, and decide, in the long hours before dawn, what he saw and what he did not. In Jefferson, the stores along Front Street keep their own watch, the jail keeps another, and the ore sheds at the edge of town keep a third. These men know one another by boot-fall and cough. They share tobacco at the corners where their routes cross, and they trade intelligence the way other men trade scrip — carefully, and never for free.

Their authority is the width of a lantern's throw and no wider. Beyond that circle, the town belongs to whoever is willing to stand in the dark. A watchman who lasts more than a season learns which doors stay locked because the owner is cautious and which stay locked because the owner has something worth hiding. He learns which alleys carry sound and which swallow it. He learns that reputation is the only weapon that works without powder, and that reputation is spent faster than it is earned.

### Role

Town-patrol figure who guards property, watches doors, and decides what the night's report will say.

### Traits

- Walks the same route so many nights the dogs no longer bark at him
- Carries a lantern in one hand and keeps the other free
- Speaks softly to drunks and loudly to strangers
- Sleeps in a back room he will not name

### Trail Work

#### The Rounds

Walk the patrol route at the usual hour. Any door found open, any window broken, any shadow out of place — note it or ignore it, but the choice belongs to the watchman. A regular round keeps merchants trusting. A skipped round invites questions no one wants asked in daylight.

#### Lantern Signal

Raise the lamp high at a corner post to show the street is watched. Lower it behind a hip to pass unseen. A covered lantern at a particular doorstep can mean all clear, stay away, or come now, depending on the arrangement. The signal is only as reliable as the man holding the flame.

#### Key and Padlock

Carry keys to certain doors — the jail, the assay office, the feed warehouse. Each key is a leash. Whoever gave you the key can take it back, and whoever sees which doors you open knows whose man you are. Losing a key is worse than losing a fight.

#### The Blind Eye

Choose not to see what was plainly there. A crate moved after hours, a man leaving a building he should not have entered, a stain on a step that was clean at sundown. The blind eye is currency. It buys peace, it buys favors, and it buys enemies who smile until the night you are no longer useful.

#### Noise in the Alley

Investigate a sound behind the buildings — a dropped crate, a scuffle, a whispered argument cut short. Going alone into a dark alley with a lantern means announcing yourself to whatever waits there. Bringing a second man means sharing what you find. Every noise is a choice between courage and sense.

#### Jail Door Watch

Stand at the jail through the small hours while the deputy sleeps. The prisoner talks. The prisoner begs. The prisoner offers money, names, threats. The watchman holds the door and holds his tongue, or he does not. What passes between a watchman and a prisoner before dawn is known only to them and to God, and God does not file reports.

#### Ore Shed Vigil

Guard the sheds where raw ore waits for the next wagon or assay. Theft from an ore shed is common enough that owners expect a percentage lost. The watchman's job is to keep that percentage reasonable. A man who guards ore long enough learns its weight by the sound a sack makes when it hits the floor, and he learns which teamsters arrive too early and leave too heavy.

#### The Witness

Be present when something happens and be asked to say what you saw. A watchman's testimony carries weight precisely because he was paid to be there. But testimony is a door that swings both ways — once you say what you saw, you cannot unsay it, and the man you named will remember your lantern for a long time.

#### Sold Silence

Accept payment to forget a face, a time, a direction of travel. Silence is the watchman's most profitable skill and his most dangerous habit. One sale makes the next one easier. Three sales make you owned. After that, the man who bought your silence owns your rounds, your keys, and your sleep.

#### Fatigue and the Fourth Hour

Fight the weight of exhaustion in the dead hours when the town is finally still. Boredom and cold conspire to close the eyes. A watchman who falls asleep on his route may wake to find nothing changed — or may wake to find that the one thing that mattered happened in the twenty minutes he could not keep his head up. Every night asks the same question, and most nights the answer does not matter, until the one night it does.

### Camp Say

> *"I know every lock on this street and every man who holds a key. I know who snores and who paces and who leaves a window cracked in January. I know all of it. But the thing that happened — the one thing — happened in the dark between my lamp and the next man's lamp, and neither of us saw a goddamn thing."*
> — meaning: authority is a circle of light, and the trouble always starts where the light stops
