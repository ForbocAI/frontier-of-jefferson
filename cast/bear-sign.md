## Black Bear Pressure

Black bear country does not announce itself with a roar. It announces itself with a turned-over cook pot, a torn flour sack, and tracks in the mud beside the creek where you washed your skillets. The bears of the Jefferson hills are not enemies. They are creditors, and every scrap of bacon grease or uncovered salt pork is a promissory note you did not mean to sign. A bear that finds a meal in your camp will return for another. A bear that returns twice owns the ground, and you are the one who will move.

These animals are strong enough to peel bark from a standing pine and patient enough to sit a berry thicket from dawn to dark. They climb what a man cannot. They smell what a man does not know he has left behind. A sow with cubs will bluff-charge a mounted horse and mean enough of it to make the horse believe her. A boar trailing a carcass will not yield the road. Most trouble with bears starts not in the wild but in the camp, where somebody left the grease can uncovered or hung the venison too low or dumped coffee grounds at the edge of the fire ring and called it clean enough.

### Role

Living consequence of poor camp discipline and careless food storage along the Jefferson hill trails.

### Traits

- **Nose Before Eyes** — A bear finds food by scent from a quarter mile or better. It does not need to see your camp to know what you cooked last night.
- **Learner of Habit** — A bear fed once by human error will test every camp it finds thereafter. The animal remembers what worked.
- **Bluff and Commit** — A black bear will pop its jaw, huff, and stamp before it charges. Most charges are warnings. The ones that are not give you no second chance to guess.

### Signs and Pressures

#### Raided Stores

A cache torn open overnight. Flour on the ground, tin lids bent, rope gnawed through. The bear is gone but the damage says it will be back. The outfit must decide what can be salvaged, what must be buried, and whether to move camp before dark.

#### Cub Sighting

Two cubs in a meadow near the trail, wrestling over a rotten log full of grubs. The sow is nearby but not yet visible. Any rider who pushes forward risks putting himself between mother and young, and no amount of steady horsemanship will make that safe.

#### Tracks at the Creek

Fresh prints in the sand where the riders water their stock. The bear is using the same creek, probably at night. Whoever draws water at dusk or dawn may meet it face to face, and a surprised bear at close quarters does not bluff.

#### Honey Tree

A dead pine torn apart halfway up, comb and wax scattered below, bees still working the wreckage. The bear may be denned nearby or may return at evening. Good camp can be made here, but only if the riders are willing to share the ground and keep every crumb locked away.

#### Bluff Charge

A bear rises on its hind legs at the far edge of a clearing, then drops and rushes twenty yards before stopping. Horses bolt. Packs come loose. Nobody is hurt, but the outfit is scattered and half the gear is in the brush. The bear watches from the tree line, chewing something it found in a dropped saddlebag.

#### Livestock Worry

A rancher's pigs have been raided three nights running. The sow pen is torn open each time, and one shoat is missing. The rancher wants the bear killed. The bear is only doing what a bear does when someone pens easy meat at the edge of timber country. Killing it may bring the next bear faster if the pen is not rebuilt.

#### Carcass Claim

A dead elk in a ravine, half-buried under leaves and dirt. The bear has claimed it and will defend the cache. The trail passes within thirty yards, and there is no good detour without losing half a day. Riders must choose between a dangerous passage and a long delay.

#### Garbage Bear

A bear that has been into the settlement dump three seasons running now walks the main road after dark and does not spook at lantern light. It has lost its fear of people. It broke a cabin door last week. The town wants it driven off but nobody wants to be the one standing in the road when it decides not to leave.

#### Berry Patch Stand-Off

The riders need to cross a slope thick with ripe huckleberry. A bear is feeding there, unhurried, ears forward, aware of the horses but unwilling to abandon the best meal it has had in weeks. Waiting costs daylight. Pressing costs nerve. Going around costs the only good water for miles.

#### Winter Den Mistake

A line shack or root cellar opened in early spring reveals a bear denned inside, groggy and foul-tempered. The animal is between the riders and the supplies they came for. It is not fully awake and not fully asleep, and that makes it less predictable than a bear in summer.

### Camp Say

> "A fed bear is a dead bear, only it don't know it yet and neither does the fool who left the bacon out. You solve a bear problem the same way you got one — with what you did or didn't do in camp."

*Meaning: Bear trouble is camp trouble. Fix the camp and the bear fixes itself. Fail to, and you will answer for it on the trail or at the settlement gate.*
