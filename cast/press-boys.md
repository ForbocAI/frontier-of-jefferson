## Press Boys

> *"Wet ink don't wait for nobody's permission. You fold it, you run it, and whatever name's on that front page — well, that name belongs to the street now."*

## Ink Runners

Every morning before the courthouse clock strikes six, the back door of the Jefferson *Sentinel* opens and a stack of still-damp broadsheets hits a wooden crate. The boys who carry them are not employed in any formal sense. They are paid in pennies, fed in scraps, and trusted with the single most dangerous commodity in a small town: other people's business set in movable type. They know the compositor's moods, the editor's debts, and which names got pulled from the proof at the last hour. Some of them can read. All of them can remember.

They are not children playing at work. They are the last mile between a linotype slug and a man's ruined reputation. A press boy who drops a bundle in the mud has wasted paper. A press boy who reads tomorrow's headline to the wrong ear at the wrong saloon has started something that cannot be taken back. They run barefoot or in boots too large, hands black to the wrist, and they know every back alley, every kitchen door, and every widow who tips a nickel for yesterday's edition. The paper is their livelihood. The gossip is their currency. The difference between the two is thinner than the newsprint itself.

### Role

Carriers of public truth and private ruin, one folded sheet at a time.

### Traits

- Fingers permanently stained past the second knuckle, and a habit of reading everything upside-down and backward from years beside the press
- Can tell you who visited the editor's office on Tuesday before the editor himself remembers
- Moves through a crowd like a dropped coin — fast, low, and hard to catch
- Sleeps on bundled returns in the press room and wakes at the sound of the typesetter's hammer

### Trail Work

#### The Early Edition

A press boy can get a headline into the right hands before the ink is dry, giving someone time to act, flee, or prepare a denial. The cost is that once even one sheet leaves the shop early, the boy has chosen a side — and the editor will notice the short count.

#### Overheard at the Stone

Standing beside the composing stone where type is locked into the chase, a press boy hears names, figures, accusations, and retractions spoken aloud as the compositor reads back copy. Repeating what was heard there is a betrayal of the shop. Not repeating it, when the right person asks, might be worse.

#### The Smudged Proof

A galley proof with a critical name blurred beyond reading can be an accident or a favor. Press boys who "accidentally" drag a sleeve across wet type have saved men from public shame and earned debts that no ledger records. But a smudge only works once before the foreman starts watching hands.

#### Who Bought the Silence

When a story disappears between the evening proof and the morning run, someone paid for that absence. The press boy knows who came to the back door after hours, what envelope changed hands, and which column now holds a patent-medicine advertisement where a name used to be. That knowledge is worth more than the pennies he was paid to forget it.

#### The Correction Box

A retraction printed in small type on page four can undo a man or save one. A press boy who knows the difference between a correction demanded by a lawyer and one demanded by a conscience can trade that distinction for favor. The danger is that both kinds look the same on paper, and guessing wrong makes an enemy of whoever needed the truth kept quiet.

#### Runaway Gossip

A headline read aloud at a crossroads moves faster than any horse. Once a press boy shouts a name on a street corner, the news belongs to the wind. He can choose where to start shouting — near the livery, the saloon, the church steps — and the direction of the rumor changes who hears it first and who hears it last. The boy who chooses well eats. The boy who chooses poorly runs.

#### The Fold and the Hold

Not every sheet in the bundle is the same. A press boy can fold a particular copy with a particular story facing out, or bury it deep where only someone who reads past the advertisements will find it. Placement is power. A damning article folded inward and sold to a distracted buyer might as well not exist until someone else points it out.

#### Printer's Debt

The press room runs on credit — ink, paper, lead, and labor all owed to someone. A press boy who runs errands for the creditors knows exactly how close the paper is to shutting down, and who holds the note. That boy can carry a message of mercy or a message of collection, and the editor may never know which one was actually sent.

#### Hidden Kindness

Sometimes a press boy leaves a clean copy on a doorstep not because he was paid to, but because the widow inside taught him his letters, or the old man on the porch once gave him shoes. These small deliveries are acts of loyalty in a trade that runs on betrayal. But kindness has a shape, and someone watching the route will notice where the boy slows down and why.

#### The Name on the Street

When a press boy learns a headline before anyone else, he carries a lit match in a town made of dry wood. He can warn the named party, sell the warning, or simply watch what happens when the morning edition hits the stands. Every choice costs something. Warning a guilty man makes you complicit. Warning an innocent one makes you a target. Saying nothing makes you the only person in town who knew and did not act, and small towns remember that kind of silence.

### Camp Say

> *"Folks talk about freedom of the press like it's something grand and constitutional. Down here it means a twelve-year-old with black hands and no shoes knows your business before you do, and he'll sell it for a penny to anyone standing on the corner. That's your free press. That's your republic. God help us all, it works."*
