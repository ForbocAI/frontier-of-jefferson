## Stove Women and Breakfast Men

Every mining camp that lasts more than a season grows a boardinghouse, and every boardinghouse worth its chimney smoke runs on the back of a cook who sleeps three hours and hears everything. They are the ones who know which man didn't come down for breakfast and which one came down twice. They know who pays on time, who begs credit, who slips coin to another man's wife, and who coughs blood into his napkin when he thinks nobody is watching. A boardinghouse cook feeds forty mouths a day and remembers every one of them by what they owe, what they eat, and what they said when they thought the kitchen couldn't hear.

They are not soft people. They haul water before dawn, split kindling in the dark, and stretch a side of beef across a week when the supply wagon doesn't come. They give extra bread to the sick and cut portions for the cruel. A cook who likes you will save you a plate. A cook who doesn't will serve you last, and cold, and let the whole table see it. The kitchen is warmer than the town and twice as dangerous to anyone fool enough to lie at the table.

### Role

You feed people. That means you decide who eats, when they eat, and what they hear while they're chewing.

### Traits

- Hands scarred from grease and blade, always wiped on the same apron
- Knows who came home late by whose boots were muddy at the back door
- Talks low and steady, never raises voice except to call supper
- Sleeps sitting up in a chair by the stove, one ear on the house

### Trail Work

#### The First Plate

Whoever you serve first at breakfast, the whole table notices. Serving a man first is a public endorsement. Serving him last is a public wound. You can shift a man's standing in this town without saying a single word — just by where you set the plate.

#### Kitchen Ears

Men talk freely when they smell bacon. You hear claim disputes, debt confessions, love arrangements, and murder plans — all before the eggs are done. What you repeat and to whom is worth more than most men earn in a week.

#### The Extra Portion

When a man is sick, broke, or hiding, you can slip him a plate through the back door. Nobody questions a cook carrying food. This makes you the best smuggler in town, and the only one who smells like biscuits.

#### Spoiled Stores

You know when the meat has turned, when the flour has weevils, when the water tastes like copper. You can warn the house and be a hero, or you can serve it quiet and let a particular table get particular cramps. Either way, you know what's in the pot and nobody else does.

#### The Pantry Door

Your pantry is the one room nobody enters without permission. A person can hide in there for a day, maybe two, behind the sacks of oats and the vinegar barrel. You have sheltered runaway wives, wanted men, and children who needed one night where nobody hit them. The pantry is yours.

#### Breakfast Debt

You keep a ledger in your head — who owes for meals, who paid ahead, who traded labor for a plate. When a man owes you three weeks of breakfasts, you own a piece of his pride. You can forgive the debt publicly and make a friend for life, or you can announce it at the table and make him small.

#### The Kitchen Knife

You are never unarmed. You carry a boning knife on your belt and a cleaver within reach. No one who has watched you break down a elk carcass before sunrise will ever mistake you for helpless. A boardinghouse cook does not need a pistol. A boardinghouse cook has a eight-inch blade and knows where the joints are.

#### The Sick Room

When someone falls ill — fever, mining cough, bad water — they end up in your care, because the doctor costs money and you have broth. You sit with the dying. You make the tea that breaks fevers. You are the last face some men see, and the first face they see when they come back. That buys loyalty no gold can match.

#### Quiet Alliances

You trade with the butcher, the grocer, the egg woman, the man who brings firewood. These are small dealings, but they are constant, and they make you the center of a web of daily commerce that the big men in town never notice. When you need a favor — a message carried, a door left open, a name kept quiet — you call on the people who bring you salt and lard, and they come through.

#### Who Made the Kitchen Go Quiet

Sometimes a name is spoken at the table and your hands stop moving. The whole kitchen hears the silence. That name belongs to someone who wronged you, or wronged someone under your roof, or owes a debt so ugly the stove itself seems to cool. What you do next — whether you resume cooking or set down the spoon and turn around — tells the table everything it needs to know.

### Camp Say

> *"She fed me when I had nothing and she never once mentioned it. That is the kind of debt that don't go on a ledger. That goes on your bones."*

A person who controls breakfast controls the first truth of the day.
