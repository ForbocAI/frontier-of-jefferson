## Timber Bosses

> *"Any man who signs on with my crew will be paid what I promised, fed what I can afford, and buried where he falls. That's the contract. You want better terms, go clerk for a dry-goods store."*
> — Stump notice nailed to a sugar pine, Camp Redtail, spring 1899

## Saw Camp Foremen

The timber boss is the man who turns standing forest into stacked lumber and stacked lumber into cash money. He holds the cutting contract, hires the crew, marks the trees, builds the skid road, and settles every argument that starts between the first axe stroke and the last load down the mountain. Some own their outfits outright. Others run crews on behalf of mill syndicates in Yreka or Redding, drawing a percentage and answering letters they'd rather burn. Either way, the boss is the law above the snow line. He sets the pace. He picks who eats, who sleeps dry, who gets docked, and who gets carried out on a plank.

What separates a timber boss from a common bucksaw hand is the ledger. He knows the board-foot yield of a slope before the first tree drops. He knows what the railroad will pay per tie, what the mine will pay per stull timber, what the rancher will pay per fence post — and he knows, down to the half-dollar, what it costs to keep a ten-man crew cutting through mud season. The mountain doesn't negotiate, but everybody else does, and the timber boss sits at the middle of every handshake that smells like pitch and sawdust. His roads cross ranch gates, reservation boundaries, mine claims, and town lots. Wherever the logs go, so does his trouble.

### Role

The one who decides what gets cut, who does the cutting, and what the mountain owes him for the privilege.

### Traits

- Hands mapped with white scars from cable splinters and bark slabs; fingers that count money and timber with equal speed
- Speaks in board feet, day wages, and road grades — measures everything, including silence
- Carries a tally book in oilcloth and a short pencil behind one ear; the book is never offered for inspection
- Sleeps lighter than any man in camp and walks the skid road before dawn, listening for what the night shifted

### Trail Work

#### The Cutting Contract

A timber boss can produce a signed contract giving him rights to fell trees on a particular slope — but the contract may not say what he claims it says, and the man who signed it may not have owned what he sold. When the boss waves that paper, somebody at the table must decide whether to read it close or take his word.

#### Skid Road Law

The boss controls the skid road — the muddy, greased-log track that moves timber downhill to the mill or the river. Whoever holds the road holds the only way off the mountain for logs, men, and mules. He can open it, close it, or charge toll, and every ranch, mine, and settlement downslope feels the choice.

#### Crew Silence

When a man gets hurt in camp — a limb-loss, a crush, a fall from a spar tree — the boss decides what story travels down the mountain. He can report it honest, bury the details in his ledger, or tell the injured man's family a version that keeps the contract clean. The crew knows what happened. The crew says nothing, because the crew still wants to eat.

#### Bunkhouse Discipline

Ten men in a log bunkhouse through a wet spring will find every reason to kill each other. The timber boss keeps order not through fairness but through the pay dock — short a man's wages for fighting, drinking, breaking tools, or talking to the wrong visitor. The dock is written in the tally book, and the tally book is gospel.

#### The Timber Thief

Not every log that falls on a contracted slope belongs to the man who holds the contract. Some trees stand on disputed ground — reservation timber, railroad grant land, a rancher's unproved claim. The boss may cut them anyway and mark them as his own, or he may discover that someone else has been cutting his trees at night and floating them downstream before the tally man arrives.

#### Slope Scars

After a crew strips a hillside, the rain comes and the soil goes. Mud slides close roads, fill creek beds, flood downstream claims, and bury whatever was planted below. The timber boss knows this will happen. He has seen it before. The question is whether he will be in the county when it does, or whether he will have moved his crew to the next drainage and left his name on nothing but stumps.

#### Road Bargain

A timber boss building a skid road through contested ground must negotiate passage — with the rancher whose fence line he crosses, with the mine whose water ditch he diverts, with the Native families whose fish camp sits where he needs a log landing. Each agreement is a handshake or a threat, and each one can be broken by the next rain or the next dollar.

#### Pay Day and Short Count

Crew wages come at the end of the month or the end of the contract, whichever the boss prefers. He can pay fair, pay short, or pay in company scrip redeemable only at a store he owns a share in. A man who argues the count argues it once. If the boss says sixty days were fifty-eight, then fifty-eight it is — unless the whole crew decides to argue together.

#### The Hidden Tally

The boss keeps two sets of numbers: one for the mill buyer or the syndicate man who reviews the contract, and one for himself. The hidden tally tracks what was actually cut, what was actually paid, what was lost to breakage or theft, and what was buried — timber and otherwise. If someone finds the real book, the boss loses more than money.

#### Town Leverage

When the timber boss comes down from the mountain, he brings payroll money and crew thirst into a town that needs both. He can direct his men to spend at one saloon and not another, buy supplies from one store and boycott the next, hire local teamsters or bring in his own. The town needs his dollars. The town does not need his trouble. That tension is the only card he plays, and he plays it every season.

### Camp Say

> *"He told me once, 'I don't hate a single tree on this mountain. I just know what each one is worth standing and what it's worth on the ground, and the difference is my living.' I believed him. I also noticed he never once looked up at the hillside after his crew was done with it."*
> — A teamster who hauled for three seasons out of Camp Redtail, recalling the foreman over winter whiskey
