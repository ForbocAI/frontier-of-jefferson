## Prologue

![Prologue](prologue/images/prologue.jpg)

> The road book for Shasta County, 1899, page forty-one: "French Gulch payroll delayed — weather, or worse. Rider sent. No word back."

Rain has been falling on French Gulch for three days. The creek is high and brown with mine tailings, and the wagon road south toward Shasta is belly-deep mud in places where it was passable a week ago. The Friday payroll for the Washington Mine did not arrive. The company store clerk has extended the last credit he means to extend, and the miners who were patient on Saturday are not patient on Monday. Someone cut the saddle cinch on the payroll rider's horse — cut it clean, with a knife, not worn through by trail — and the rider turned back to Shasta rather than risk the ford on a bad rig. That is the story. Whether it is the whole story is a question the town is already answering in four or five different ways.

The posse arrives into this. Maybe they were hired to escort the next payroll attempt. Maybe they owe the company store themselves and came to work it off. Maybe they are just riders who stopped in French Gulch for the night and cannot leave because the creek ford is too high to cross safely. However they came, they are here now, and French Gulch has a way of putting a name to every stranger before the stranger has finished stabling the horse.

### What The Posse Knows

- The Friday payroll from Shasta to the Washington Mine is at least three days overdue.
- The creek ford south of town is high and dangerous — a loaded wagon will not make it without risk.
- The company store has stopped extending credit, and the miners are restless.
- A payroll rider turned back after finding his saddle cinch cut. He reported it to the Shasta sheriff, not the French Gulch constable.
- Wintu families camped along Clear Creek upstream have been quiet, but their fish traps are set and their fires are burning — they are watching the road and the water both.
- A torn freight tag was found nailed to the sutler-post door with no load to match it.

### What The Town Hides

- The company store clerk keeps a second ledger that does not match the first.
- A hard-rock miner who quit the Washington last month has been seen on the south road at odd hours, always alone, always armed.
- The French Gulch constable owes the mining company fourteen months of back favors and has not filed a report on the cut cinch.
- Karuk traders who came down from the Klamath crossing three days ago left town faster than their business should have required. They spoke to the Wintu families but not to the store.
- A horse that belongs to no one in town is tied behind the livery stable. It carries a Shasta County brand and an empty saddle scabbard.

### First Night In French Gulch

The rain will not stop tonight. The posse can stable their horses at the livery for credit or coin, eat beans and salt pork at the boardinghouse if the cook has not run out of both, and sleep in the loft above the freight office if they do not mind the smell of wet canvas and axle grease. The saloon is open but it is not friendly — the miners who have not been paid drink on the slate, and the slate is full. The company store is locked after dark. The constable is somewhere, or he is not. Before the posse sleeps, they will hear at least two versions of why the payroll did not come, and neither version will mention the same names. By morning, someone will have asked them to ride south, and someone else will have warned them not to.

### Road Out

> The mud does not care whose side you are on. The creek does not wait for the payroll. The road south holds an answer, and the road north holds another, and French Gulch sits between them collecting interest on both. Saddle up or settle in — the ledger is already open to your page.
