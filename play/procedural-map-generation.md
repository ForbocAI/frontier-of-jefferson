## Procedural Map Generation

![Procedural Map Generation](play/images/procedural-map-generation.jpg)

> *From a surveyor's notebook found in a French Gulch boarding house, the binding cracked and the margins full of arithmetic:*
>
> *"Drew the county from memory. Got the creeks right, the ridges close, and the roads wrong in a way that will probably kill somebody. Good enough for government work."*

### Map First Principles

The map is not the territory. It is a tool for making decisions cost something.

Every place on the map exists to create pressure. A settlement without trouble is a settlement without purpose. A road without risk is a road nobody remembers. A ridge without something on the other side is wasted ink. When you build the map, build it like a county clerk with a grudge — everything on the page should owe somebody money, shelter, blood, or silence.

Start with French Gulch. It is the first anchor: an ore town on Clear Creek, small enough to know every face and busy enough to lose one. From French Gulch, the map grows outward along the routes that people actually travel — the freight road south toward Shasta, the pack trail west into the Trinity drainage, the ridge paths north toward the Klamath, and the creek bottoms east toward the Sacramento. Do not draw the whole county at once. Draw what the riders can reach in a hard day, and let the edges stay rough.

### Roads, Ridges, Water, and Claims

**Roads.** Every road on the map needs a name, a condition, and an owner. The name is what people call it — Whiskey Creek Road, the Shasta Freight Road, the old Company Trail. The condition is what it costs to travel — Open, Soft, Washed, Blocked, or Watched. The owner is whoever profits from it, patrols it, or neglects it. If nobody owns the road, somebody is about to.

**Ridges.** Ridges divide the country into pockets. A ridge between two settlements means those settlements might be a day apart by trail but a week apart by wagon. Ridges hide things — camps, claims, timber operations, people who do not want to be found. Put a ridge on the map and you have created a wall that riders must choose to cross or ride around.

**Water.** Every creek, river, and spring is a lifeline. Water means camp, fish, drinking stock, and passage — or it means flood, washout, and delay. Name the crossings. A ford that runs knee-deep in summer may run chest-deep after rain, and a bridge that holds a rider may not hold a loaded wagon. Water is the frontier's clock: follow it downstream to reach people, follow it upstream to leave them behind.

**Claims.** A claim is a piece of ground someone has declared theirs — by law, by bluff, or by shovel. Mining claims cluster along the creeks. Timber claims climb the ridges. Ranching claims spread across the flats. Every claim on the map should carry a name, a resource, and a dispute. If a claim has no dispute, it has no story.

### People Before Empty Terrain

No place on the map should be empty. Even the most remote ridge has someone on it or someone who was there last season.

For every location the table generates, assign at least one of the following: a person who lives there, a person who left there, a group that claims it, or a group that is pushing into it. The Wintu families of Clear Creek have been on this land longer than any mine. The Karuk families hold the Klamath drainage. Ranchers are pushing into the meadows. Timber companies are cutting the ridges. The company runs the freight roads, and the county runs the courthouse — when it bothers to.

A place with people has friction. A place with two groups of people has a story. A place with three groups and one water source has a whole session.

### Weather and Animal Pressure

The country does not sit still while riders make plans.

**Weather.** Every session should open with a weather word — Clear, Fog, Rain, Snow, Wind, or Heat. That word touches everything: road condition, travel time, camp comfort, visibility, temper, and trade. Rain turns a one-day ride into a two-day slog and a passable ford into a drowning hazard. Snow closes the passes and traps anyone on the wrong side of a ridge. Fog hides riders from each other and from the law. Heat dries creeks, kills stock, and starts fires in the pine stands. Weather is the cheapest complication and the hardest to argue with.

**Animals.** Bear, cougar, deer, elk, and salmon are not background. They are map pressure. A bear in camp costs provisions. A cougar on the trail costs nerve and daylight. A deer herd on the move tells you the season is turning. Salmon in the creek mean the Wintu and Karuk families are fishing — and have been fishing here since before the mines existed. Place animal sign on the map the way you place roads: where they create decisions.

### Rolling A Day Ride

When the riders leave a settlement and head for the next named place on the map, the day ride is what happens between departure and arrival.

1. **Set the weather.** Choose or draw a weather word for the day. Apply it to road condition and travel time.
2. **Name the road.** Confirm the road's current condition — Open, Soft, Washed, Blocked, or Watched. If the condition has changed since last crossing, say so.
3. **Spend something.** The ride costs food, water, horse care, daylight, or patience. Deduct from what the riders are carrying. If they are carrying nothing, the ride costs more.
4. **Drop a sign.** Place one piece of animal sign, weather sign, or human sign along the route — bear tracks at a creek crossing, a fresh blaze on a tree, smoke from a camp that should be empty, a rider on the ridge who turns away when spotted.
5. **Arrive or camp.** If the destination is within the day's reach, the riders arrive and face whatever is waiting. If it is not, they must choose a camp site — and every camp site has a cost, a comfort, and a risk.
6. **Close the day.** At camp or at the destination, something small changes: a rumor arrives, a provision runs low, a horse throws a shoe, a stranger asks a question. The day is never clean.

### Map Marks

> *The old map has been folded so many times the creases have worn through. Someone has drawn over the printed roads in heavy pencil — corrections, most of them, though a few look more like warnings.*
>
> *Mark what matters. Leave the rest to the country. It will fill itself in whether you draw it or not.*
