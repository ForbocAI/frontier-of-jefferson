# Frontier of Jefferson — A Western Tabletop Packet

![Frontier of Jefferson](introduction/images/frontier-of-jefferson.jpg)

> The road north from Shasta forks at Clear Creek, and whichever branch you ride, someone already wrote your name in a ledger before you arrived. This is how the frontier keeps its books.

## What This Packet Is

**A tabletop packet for grounded western play set in the contested Jefferson State frontier, 1899.**

Written and directed by Sean Dinwiddie.

In accordance with the county road books, claim-office records, oral memory of the Wintu and Karuk peoples, and the hard testimony of those who worked, rode, bled, and argued over the land between Clear Creek and the Klamath.

# Introduction

`FOJ-WD-001 — World Document: Jefferson State Frontier, 1899`

> You will come into French Gulch the way everyone comes in — tired, owing something, and a day behind the last honest report from the road.

The year is 1899 and the frontier called Jefferson State has not decided what it wants to be. The Klamath mountains hold the northern wall. Clear Creek cuts the middle country into gulches where men have pulled gold, copper, and silver for fifty years and have not yet agreed on who owns the water that runs through the tailings. The old Wintu families who fished and burned and gathered acorns along these creeks before any mining camp had a name are still here — living, working, trading, holding their ground with the weight of generations against courthouse filings that often contradict each other.

French Gulch sits at the pressure point. It is a mining settlement on Clear Creek where the ore wagons load, the company store extends credit it means to collect, and the saloon keeps a slate board of debts that doubles as a map of who can be trusted. The stage road connects Shasta to the south — brick ruins, iron shutters, a courthouse that still processes claims as though the town had not already half-burned — and the freight trails running north toward the Klamath River and the Oregon line. Every rider, miner, packer, clerk, and hand who passes through French Gulch leaves a mark in somebody's ledger.

The law here is a patchwork of county sheriff authority, company interests, and the older territorial understandings that Native families and long-settled ranchers carry in memory and habit. A man may hold a legal claim on paper and still find his fence posts pulled up by someone whose family ran cattle on that ground before the county office existed. A Wintu woman may know exactly where the salmon run thin and why, and the mining company will not ask her because asking would mean admitting the creek is damaged. A freight driver may carry a rifle under the seat because the road between French Gulch and the Klamath crossing has a reputation that the sheriff cannot patrol away.

This is not a place of simple heroes and villains. The people who live here — the ford men who know every crossing, the fence riders who check wire in the rain, the hard-rock miners who cough dust and collect scrip, the Karuk families who fish the Klamath and trade downriver, the Hoopa Valley packers who move goods on trails the wagons cannot reach, the Pit River hands who hire out for ranch work and remember land that was taken — all of them share the same difficult country. Their debts cross. Their rumors travel the same mud roads. Their children hear the same coyotes at night.

What you bring to the table is a posse: a handful of people with horses, debts, skills, reputations, and reasons to ride. The packet gives you the land, the people, the pressures, and the tools to play out whatever trouble finds you on the road between French Gulch and the far ridges. Whether you haul ore honestly, run a claim dispute through the courthouse, track a cougar that has been killing stock, or settle a freight-road argument with a rifle, the frontier will remember what you did and who saw you do it.

## Old Marks of Arrival

> *Blaze on a pine at the Clear Creek fork — hatchet cut, pitch-sealed, facing the trail.* It means someone came this way and wanted the next rider to know the road holds.

> *Iron ring spiked into bridge timber at the wagon crossing.* It means the bridge was paid for, and whoever paid expects the toll.

> *Three stones stacked at a cold camp above the gulch.* It means the camp was used and left clean, and the next traveler is welcome to the fire ring but not to the cache buried nearby.

> *A torn freight tag nailed to a sutler-post door.* It means the load did not arrive whole, and someone down the road has an answer to give.
