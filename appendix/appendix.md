### Names and Places

A facilitator at the table should never stall for a name. The frontier runs on introductions, and every settlement, crossroads, claim, and camp needs a person attached to it — someone who owns it, lost it, wants it, or left it behind.

**Western Given Names.** Keep a short list within reach. Pull from county records and headstones of the period: names like Eula, Virgil, Orrin, Cora, Festus, Pearl, Lemuel, Nettie. Mix plain and particular. Avoid names that belong to any single famous figure of the era.

**Settlement Tags.** Every settlement the table visits should carry a single word that tells you what it runs on — Ore, Timber, Freight, Cattle, Law, or Debt. That word shapes who is there, what they need, and what they will fight over. French Gulch is Ore. The next town north might be Timber. A settlement with no tag is a settlement with no purpose, and riders will pass through it without stopping.

**Road Tags.** Every named road or trail should carry a condition: Open, Soft, Washed, Blocked, or Watched. The condition changes with the season and with whoever controls the nearest bridge or ford. A road that was Open in September may be Washed by November and Watched by a company patrol by spring.

**Work Tags.** Every person the table meets should carry an occupation that tells you what they can do and what they need. Packer, Cook, Assayer, Clerk, Drover, Sawyer, Farrier, Surveyor, Midwife, Preacher, Barkeep. The work tells you the person's leverage and their weakness.

### Work, Weather, and Wants

The frontier economy is blunt. People need food, shelter, ammunition, tools, and animals. Everything else is luxury, and luxury is leverage.

**Jobs and Pay.** A day's labor in the mines pays in company scrip more often than coin. Scrip spends at the company store and nowhere else. Real money moves through freight contracts, cattle sales, timber bids, and whatever the assay office says the dust is worth this week. When the table needs a price, keep it rough: a meal costs a quarter, a horse costs more than most people earn in a month, and a bribe costs whatever the bribed man thinks he can get away with.

**Scarcity.** Pick one thing the current settlement is short of — nails, coffee, lamp oil, horseshoes, writing paper, whiskey, clean water, salt, or rifle cartridges. That shortage tells you what people will trade for, what they will steal, and what a clever rider can haul in from the next town for a profit.

**Weather Pressure.** The northern California mountains do not forgive bad timing. Rain turns roads to clay. Snow closes passes. Summer heat dries creeks and starts fires in the pine stands. Every session should carry a weather word — Clear, Fog, Rain, Snow, Wind, Heat — and that word should touch travel, camp, and trade. A rider who ignores the weather will lose a horse, a day, or worse.

**Travel Cost.** A day's ride between settlements should always cost something: food from the pack, water from the canteen, shoe leather from the horse, patience from the company. If the table moves between towns without spending anything, the map is too generous.

### Animals and Track Sign

The country between French Gulch and the Klamath is shared with animals that do not recognize claim stakes.

**Bear Sign.** Black bear are common in the oak and pine belt. They follow food — acorn mast in autumn, camp garbage year-round, salmon runs when the creeks are high. A bear in camp means lost provisions. Claw marks on a tree, overturned stones, torn canvas — these are signs a facilitator can drop into any camp scene. Bears are not monsters; they are hungry neighbors with better noses than any hound.

**Cougar Country.** Mountain lions hunt alone, follow deer and livestock, and avoid crowds. A rider alone on a ridge trail at dusk is in cougar country. Missing livestock, drag marks in the brush, a silence where birds should be singing — these are warnings. A cougar at the table is not a combat encounter; it is a pressure that makes solo travel cost attention and nerve.

**Deer, Elk, and Salmon.** These are food, and food is survival. Deer browse the oak flats. Elk move through the higher meadows in season. Salmon fill Clear Creek and the Klamath tributaries when the runs come. Hunting and fishing feed a camp but take time, and time on the frontier is never free. Every hour spent hunting is an hour not spent watching the road.

**Restraint.** The animals of this country are not set dressing. Overhunting a creek empties it. Shooting near a settlement draws attention. Taking more than a camp needs wastes time and meat and reputation. The table should treat game the way the country does — as a resource that answers to season, weather, and use.

### Native Communities and Respectful Use

The land the riders cross is not empty. It was not empty before the mines opened, and it is not empty now.

**Living Families.** The Wintu families of Clear Creek, the Karuk families of the Klamath, the Pit River peoples of the northeast plateau, and the Hoopa Valley communities of the Trinity watershed are present in this landscape as neighbors, traders, workers, diplomats, and survivors of tremendous displacement. They are not historical relics. They are people with knowledge, obligations, grievances, and agency.

**Land and Stewardship.** Native communities have deep knowledge of fire, water, fish, game, plants, trails, and weather. This knowledge is practical and earned over generations. It is not mystical. When the table encounters Native characters, those characters should have specific knowledge, specific stakes, and specific relationships to the land and to the encroaching settlements — not vague spiritual wisdom offered from the margins.

**What Not to Invent.** Do not invent sacred ceremonies, religious practices, secret rituals, or spiritual language for any tribal community. Do not present Native characters as tragic vanishing figures, wise mystics, or nameless obstacles. If you do not know a specific cultural detail, leave the space open rather than filling it with invention. Silence is more respectful than fiction.

**Allotment and Pressure.** By 1899, federal allotment policy is pressing on every tribal community in northern California. Land is being divided, reassigned, and sold. Families are being displaced from ancestral places to make room for mines, ranches, and timber operations. This pressure is a living fact of the setting, not a historical aside. Characters at the table should encounter it as something happening now, with real consequences for real people.
