## Kitchen Lots

### In the Register of Back Doors, Wash Lines, and Scrap Gardens

![Kitchen Lots](regions/images/kitchen-lots.jpg)

> A cracked basin set upside-down on a fence post, catching nothing but frost.
> Bootprints cross the ash pit toward the side door, and one set does not come back.
> The back lot knows more than the boardwalk admits.

Behind the storefronts and the saloon walls, French Gulch keeps its real inventory. Kitchen lots are where dishwater hits the dirt, where onion skins pile beside ash barrels, where chickens scratch through scraps that tell a careful eye what a household can and cannot afford. Wash lines hang between posts like signals—how many shirts, whose mending is visible, what went missing off the rope overnight. A woman trading eggs at the back fence knows more about the town's debts than the man behind the counter out front. These lots are where food is stretched, where illness hides before it reaches the street, where a rider leaving town by the wrong door can slip past anyone watching the main road. Every scrap garden, every water bucket, every fence slat with a gap wide enough to pass a message through is a scene waiting for the table to notice.

Bargains made in kitchen lots do not carry receipts. A meal offered to a stranger may buy silence, a favor, or trouble that arrives next week wearing a different face. Children see who comes and goes; hired help knows whose pantry is bare and whose is locked. The ash pit holds burned letters and bottle glass. The side door lets someone out who never entered through the front. Whole-town politics pass between these fence lines in whispered trades—flour for information, shelter for labor, a name for a warning—and none of it reaches the public record unless somebody wants it to.

### Field Mark

> Where the wash line meets the fence corner and the ash barrel has not been emptied in a week, look for the path beaten through the scrub to the back alley. That is the door the town uses when it does not want to be seen using a door.
