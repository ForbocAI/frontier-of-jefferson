## French Gulch Road

### In the Register of Ruts, Grades, and Return Smoke

![French Gulch Road](regions/images/french-gulch-road.jpg)

> A broken spoke lodged upright in the mud like a marker nobody asked for.
> Fog sits in the ruts from Clear Creek to the first bridge, and the grade never forgives a short team.
> Whatever comes up this road arrives tired, and the town knows it.

The road from Clear Creek into French Gulch is not a kindness. It is a single artery of churned mud, wagon ruts cut axle-deep, and switchback grades that punish every loaded freight bed from the flatlands to the gulch. Riders move faster but announce themselves in hoof churn and dust; a man on horseback can be spotted two bends out by anyone sitting a porch with nothing better to do than count arrivals. Freight wagons crawl, and the teamsters who drive them carry news whether they mean to or not. Every broken spoke, every bridge delay, every mule gone lame on the grade is a conversation in town before the driver unhitches. The road is a witness to everything that enters, and it charges in time, in mud, and in the rumors that outpace the wheels.

There is no second route worth the name. A rider pressed for quiet might cut through timber, but the road owns the freight, the mail, the law papers, and the bodies headed for burial or boarding. Ambush bends sit where the brush crowds close and the grade slows a wagon to walking pace. Return smoke—cook fires, forge light, lamp oil—can be read from the road at dusk, and anyone watching knows how many camps are occupied between here and the last bridge. The road does not hide who travels it. It holds the cost of arrival and the evidence of departure, and it delivers both to French Gulch whether the town is ready or not.

### Field Mark

> Where the ruts fork at the old slide and the brush closes to one wagon width, count the hoof tracks in the mud. If the freshest set has no return, somebody is still ahead of you—and the town already knows it.
