## Claim Stakes

### In the Register of Split Cedar, Tin Notice, and Witness Cut

![Claim Stakes](regions/images/claim-stakes.jpg)

> A split cedar post driven into red dirt, its tin notice curled at one corner where the rain got behind the nail.
> Deer have rubbed the bark from the witness tree, and the pencil marks on the paper are running toward illegible.
> A claim stake is just a stick until a man brings a rifle to read it.

Claim stakes stand where law meets dirt, and neither one holds steady. A split cedar post with a tacked notice marks the boundary of a mining claim—or says it does, until someone drives a second post ten feet closer and nails a different name to it. The notice is written in pencil on whatever paper was at hand: a page torn from a ledger, a scrap of flour sacking, a sheet folded into a tin tobacco box and nailed through the lid. County seal wax, when it appears at all, softens in the heat and cracks in the frost. Survey chain scars on the brush show where a line was run, but survey chains measure distance, not agreement. The witness cut—a blaze on a standing tree—confirms the corner, or it did, before the weather took the bark and the next man claimed the blaze was his. Boot tracks circle every fresh stake, and somewhere in the timber, someone is watching to see whether the new post stands or falls.

Claim disputes start small and end with law papers, fists, or worse. A forged notice looks like a real one until the ink is tested or the witness is asked to swear. Old paths—Native use trails, game tracks, cattle routes—cross new boundaries without permission, and the men who staked the ground did not consult the men who walked it first. Displaced grazing pushes ranchers against miners; missing witnesses leave a claim unproven; a paper nailed upside down after rain may be carelessness or a message. Every stake in the ground is a fuse, and the table must decide who lit it. The legal dust is real—damp signatures, pencil marks, hard looks across a fence line that did not exist last month—and the courtroom, if it comes to that, sits in Shasta, a day's ride away, where the man with the better paper wins and the man without one rides home to find his stake gone.

### Field Mark

> Where the cedar post leans and the tin notice is too weathered to read from horseback, dismount and look for the witness cut on the nearest standing tree. If the blaze has been widened or a second mark sits below it, two men have claimed this ground—and only one of them is still nearby.
