## Sutler Posts

### In the Register of Counter, Crates, and Road Credit

![Sutler Posts](regions/images/sutler-posts.jpg)

> A flour sack split at the seam, dusting the counter where the scale sits crooked.
> Mule tracks pool with rainwater beside the loading step, and the price board has been wiped and rewritten twice since morning.
> Need is the first customer through every door.

A sutler post stands where the road gets desperate. These are not town stores with painted signs and swept floors; they are plank counters under canvas or rough timber, stocked with whatever the last freight wagon carried and priced according to how far the next supply sits. Flour, coffee, cartridges, barrel water, rope, tack nails, axle grease, and patent medicine share shelf space with items the post-keeper does not advertise until he knows what a buyer can pay. Credit is offered and recorded in pencil in a ledger that locks with a padlock, and every name in that ledger is a lever the post-keeper holds. Road news arrives with the freight and leaves with the customers—who passed through, what they carried, what they asked for that the post did not have, and what they could not afford. A man who trades at the sutler post leaves behind a receipt of his needs, and needs are the most honest currency in Jefferson country.

Barter runs alongside coin, and neither runs clean. A miner may trade raw ore for supplies and overpay because the post-keeper sets the weight. A packer may swap labor for goods and find the terms have changed by the time the work is done. False shortages drive prices; a post-keeper who hides a case of cartridges under the counter can name his figure when the next rider comes in bleeding. Water barrels carry their own toll when the creek is fouled or the road is long. Every transaction at a sutler post carries a second cost: the information the post-keeper keeps, the favor owed, the road news traded as change. The post charges extra because it knows how far the next one is, and it never forgets a debt.

### Field Mark

> Where the crate slats are stacked beside the loading step and the price board shows a figure crossed out and raised, ask what shipment is late. The answer will tell you what the post-keeper is holding back—and what he expects to charge by nightfall.
