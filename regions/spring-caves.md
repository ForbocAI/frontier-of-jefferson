## Spring Caves

### In the Register of Seep Stone, Bucket Rope, and Cold Breath

![Spring Caves](regions/images/spring-caves.jpg)

> A limestone hollow breathing cold air from a crack no wider than a man's hand, the stone face stained green with mineral seep.
> A frayed rope hangs into the dark, the bucket at the bottom knocking against rock every time the wind shifts.
> At the table, a spring cave is water you can own, water you can poison, and water that remembers everything lowered into it.

The spring caves along the Jefferson ridges and mine-adjacent hollows are not curiosities. They are the water supply for claims, camps, and stock that have no other source in dry months. Seep stone weeps through cracks in limestone and shale, pooling in hollows that stay cold year-round and taste of iron or sulfur depending on what the rock carries. A bucket on a rope is the standard rig — a frayed hemp line tied to a stake or a dead root, lowered into dark that a candle barely reaches. The footing inside is bad: wet stone, loose gravel, clay that turns slick as grease underfoot. Lamp smoke builds against the low ceiling and stays there. Wintu families and other Native people who have lived with these drainages know which seeps run clean and which carry mineral that sickens stock, and their judgment of seasonal flow is more reliable than any company man's guess. A spring cave in disputed claim country is not just water — it is leverage. The man who controls the mouth controls who drinks, who waters horses, and who hauls buckets uphill to a dry camp a quarter-mile away.

A spring cave hides what is put into it and keeps what drips through it. A cache lowered by rope sits in cold dark that preserves and conceals: papers, coin, powder, or anything a man does not want found in his tent. Tracks vanish on wet stone — a boot that leaves a clear print on trail dust leaves nothing on a cave floor washed by seep water. Sound behaves wrong underground; a whisper carries around a bend while a shout dies flat against the near wall, and a gunshot in a cave turns deafening enough to stun the man who pulled the trigger. A poisoned bucket can sicken a whole camp, and the proof washes away with the next rise of the water table. Claim men have been known to post a guard at the cave mouth and charge for access, selling water by the bucket to camps that have no choice. A trapped animal in a spring cave — a coyote, a bear cub, a mule that slipped the rope — turns the narrow entrance into a problem that cannot be solved quietly.

### Field Mark

> Where the rock face weeps green and the rope at the mouth shows fresh hand-wear over old fray, and the air coming up tastes of iron and cold stone — that is a spring cave earning its keep, and the table should ask who filled the last bucket, who guards the entrance, and what the dark water is not telling.
