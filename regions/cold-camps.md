## Cold Camps

### In the Register of Dead Ash, Bedroll Mark, and Scattered Tin

![Cold Camps](regions/images/cold-camps.jpg)

> A coffee pot kicked onto its side, grounds spilled in a line the rain has not yet washed.
> Boot scuffs circle the ash pile, and one set leads into the brush without a return track.
> The best cold camp clue is ordinary enough to make everybody uneasy.

A cold camp is what remains after the fire goes out and the people who built it do not come back to stir it. Ash gone gray tells time: white and powdery means days, gray with charcoal chunks means hours, and warm beneath a crust of rain means the camp was abandoned in a hurry. Bedroll dents in the grass or the pine needles show how many slept and how long they stayed. Hoof marks at the picket line say how many animals stood, and whether they were shod or barefoot, packed or ridden. Coffee grounds, bean cans with the lids bent back, a broken harness strap hung on a branch—these are the ordinary leavings of a night on the road. But a tool left behind where a man would not leave it, a bedroll still spread where the rain would soak it, a pot with food still in it—these are the things that turn an empty camp into a question. Bear sign at the edges—torn bark, overturned stones, scat with berry seeds—means scavengers have already worked the site, and whatever was left is scattered further than it should be.

Cold camps sit along wagon roads, on creek bars, at timber edges, and in abandoned mining cuts where the brush has grown back enough to break the wind. A table can read the camp like a letter: boot size, animal count, direction of departure, whether the fire was doused or left to burn out, whether food was packed or abandoned. The outdoors does not forgive carelessness—wet blankets freeze, sour beans draw animals, smoke sticks to wool and carries a man's presence downwind for a quarter mile. A camp that should still be warm but is not asks who left and why and whether the leaving was chosen. A staged disappearance looks like a cold camp until someone notices what is missing instead of what is left. Every cold camp is evidence, and the trail that leads away from it is the beginning of the next scene.

### Field Mark

> Where the ash pit sits cold beside a creek bar and the bedroll dents still hold their shape, count the tracks leading out. If the boot prints outnumber the hoof prints, somebody left on foot—and in this country, a man on foot did not choose to be.
