## Campfire Rings

### In the Register of Dead Coals, Coffee Grounds, and Bedroll Dents

![Campfire Rings](regions/images/campfire-rings.jpg)

> A circle of fire-blackened stones on a pine flat, the ash still warm enough to feel through a boot sole.
> Coffee grounds scattered at the edge of the ring, and the grass pressed flat in three bedroll shapes pointing away from the smoke.
> At the table, a fire ring is a witness that cannot lie — it tells how many, how long, and whether they meant to be found.

The campfire rings along the pine flats, creek bars, and cutover timber edges around French Gulch are read like letters by anyone who knows trail country. Ash tells time: white and cold means days; gray with heat underneath means hours. The size of the coals says what wood was burned — pine pops and leaves long black sticks, oak burns down to small hard coals that hold heat into the next morning. Coffee grounds at the ring edge say someone stayed long enough to boil water. Boot scuffs around the stones show how many sat and where they faced. Horse tracks at the picket line say whether the animals were hobbled or tied, calm or restless. Bedroll dents in the grass or pine duff show who slept close to the fire for warmth and who slept apart for caution. Bacon grease on a flat stone, card corners in the ash, a whiskey bottle neck cracked off and tossed — the ring collects what men leave behind when they stop watching themselves. Bear sign at the edge means the camp food was handled carelessly, or the camp was left in a hurry, or both.

A campfire ring is also a social contract. The unwritten rule in Jefferson country is that a man may share another man's fire if he announces himself from the dark and brings something to the pot or the conversation. That rule gets tested by strangers, liars, men running from something, and men looking for someone. What gets said around a fire — rumors about payroll, claim jumps, road closings, who was seen riding where — travels faster than any newspaper and is only half as reliable. A fire at night is visible for a mile on open ground and draws anyone with eyes: deputies, drifters, company men, and animals. Smoke by day marks a camp for anyone on higher ground. The man who builds a fire is advertising his position, and the man who sits in the dark beyond the ring's light is the one the table should be asking about. What was burned in the ash — papers, rope ends, a shirt with the wrong stain — matters more than what was cooked over it.

### Field Mark

> Where the stones show seasons of black and the ash holds coffee grounds mixed with boot-heel grit, and the grass beyond the ring is pressed in shapes that say who slept and who sat up watching — that is a fire ring earning its keep, and the table should ask who shared the warmth, what was said in the dark, and what the coals are still hiding.
