## Trail Blazes

### In the Register of Axe Cut, Bent Nail, and Chalk Smear

![Trail Blazes](regions/images/trail-blazes.jpg)

> A pine trunk scarred with a hatchet blaze, the cut face weathered gray at the edges and still pale where someone freshened it last season.
> A bent nail driven into a fence post at a fork, pointing left, and a chalk arrow on the rock below it already half-washed by rain.
> At the table, a trail blaze is a message that can be trusted, altered, or erased — and the man who follows it had better know which.

The trail blazes across Jefferson back country are working marks, not art. A hatchet cut on a pine trunk at eye height, bark stripped to show the pale wood underneath, means the trail goes this direction and somebody thought it mattered enough to say so. A cairn — three or four stones stacked on a flat rock at a fork — means the same, but stones can be kicked apart by a boot or scattered by a horse and the message dies with them. Bent nails in fence posts, charcoal arrows on rock faces, cut brush piled to one side of a narrow trail, branded stakes driven at claim boundaries — every mark has a maker, and every maker had a reason. Logging crews mark their skid roads so the teamsters can find the way back. Miners mark the trails to their claims so that a partner can follow but a stranger cannot read the sign without local knowledge. A chalk smear on a rock face washes away in the first hard rain, which means it was either put there recently or it was meant to be temporary. A hatchet blaze on a pine trunk lasts years, darkening and splitting with the seasons, and a man who knows wood can judge the age of the cut by the color of the exposed grain and the depth of the weathering at the edges.

A false blaze is the cheapest lie in Jefferson country. A man who wants to send pursuit down the wrong fork cuts a fresh blaze on the wrong tree, stacks a cairn at the wrong turn, or drives a nail pointing the wrong direction. The trick works once, maybe twice, before the man following starts checking the marks against the tracks on the ground — and tracks do not lie the way blazes can. Snowmelt hides blazes on low rock and exposes them on high trunks, changing which marks are readable by season. A mark meant as a warning — a slash across a blaze, a cairn knocked flat, charcoal crossed out — means the route is closed, dangerous, or claimed by someone who does not welcome traffic. The man who can read trail blazes knows direction, age, maker's habit, and intent. The man who cannot read them follows the mark and hopes. At the table, the difference between those two men is the difference between arriving and disappearing.

### Field Mark

> Where the pine shows a hatchet scar weathered gray at the edges and a chalk arrow on the rock below has faded to a ghost of itself in the rain — that is a trail blaze earning its keep, and the table should ask who cut the mark, whether anyone changed it, what the weather hid, and whether the road it points to is the road it was always meant to show.
